EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The basis of this project was to create a model of the pulmonary anatomy for a first-year resident at Tufts Medical Center, Dr. Caroline Ricard. Dr. Ricard had previously converted a CT scan into an STL file and isolated the airway, pulmonary arteries, and pulmonary veins from the rest of the anatomy. The model was printed using powder bed fusion printing technology with TPU powder and then painted. While this model provides a good visual aid to medical students and surgical residents and can help them learn pulmonary anatomy, there are several shortcomings that Dr. Ricard wanted to address through this project. The three biggest issues to be addressed included the feel or texture of the model material, hollowness of the veins and arteries, and capability of the model to be cut and sutured back together. As Dr. Ricard’s primary goal was for the model to feel similar to human anatomy, our team proposed to create the model out of either flexible/elastic resins or a soft silicone. While experimenting with these two types of materials, the team also planned to test different methods of hollowing out the model. We expected that the elastic resin model would not hold up as well to cutting and suturing as the silicone model, but planned to test the capability of both materials. The ultimate goal of this project was to deliver a pulmonary anatomy model to Dr. Ricard that was an improvement over her preliminary TPU model in some way: material texture, hollowness, or suturability. By achieving this goal, Dr. Ricard and other surgical residents could use the model to gain a better understanding of the human anatomy and prepare for future surgeries in the field of pulmonary medicine.
