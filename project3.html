EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Consumers across America are turning their attention, and their spending money, towards sustainable products. A cross-generational survey led by Southern Cross University concluded that 93% of Americans and Australians are generally concerned for the environment, with plastic pollution listed as their top concern [1]. Over the last ten years, eight states in the U.S. have banned single-use plastic bags, with some also charging a fee for using recycled paper and reusable plastic bags [2]. Packaging has become a major source of waste - per the EPA, food and packaging/containers account for nearly 45% of materials in U.S. landfills [3]. Furthermore, an analysis by the Natural Resources Defense Council (NRDC) of 47 fast food and quick service companies showed that most failed to account for the environmental impact of their packaging [4].

We know that plastic is bad for the environment, but paper packaging may just be the lesser of two evils. Biodegradable materials (including paper), take longer to decompose once disposed of in a landfill - when compacted, paper resists deterioration more than when it’s placed in a compost pile or out in the open. In fact, a study by William Rathje of the ‘Garbage Project’ showed that newspapers from 60 years ago can be intact and readable when removed from a landfill [5]. Paper bags also weigh 5-7 times the weight of plastic bags, meaning 5-7 times more waste tonnage is deposited into landfills. Furthermore, manufacturing paper bags actually consumes 2.2 times more energy and 4.7 times more water than manufacturing plastic bags [6].

To provide an alternative to paper or plastic and to capture a portion of the food packaging market, we have created the Banag: the packaging solution for quick service restaurants that communicate and embody sustainability. Our product will be fresh and natural, fully compostable, and have a low carbon footprint.

There are three primary goals for this product. The first is to develop a food packaging product utilizing banana leaves that is 100% compostable, aesthetically beautiful, cost-competitive with paper bags, and has a smaller carbon footprint than plastic and paper competitors. The second goal is to design a production method for creating banana leaf packaging. Finally, the last goal is to develop long-term customers first in sustainably-focused mom-and-pop stores, and then eventually partner with chain ‘fast-casual’ restaurants.
